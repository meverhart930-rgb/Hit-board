What the numbers mean

Hit score — our estimate of the chance this batter gets at least one hit today, calibrated to realistic hit rates (roughly the 50–82% range real hitters live in). It's an estimate, so it's best for comparing and ranking players rather than as an exact betting line.

Contact skill — the foundation, now built from two parts: how often a batter puts the ball in play (strikeouts and walks cost hit chances — this part is stable, real skill) and what happens when he does (noisier, so it leans toward league average). Two .280 hitters aren't equal: the low-strikeout one gets more swings that can become hits. The opposing pitcher is judged the same way — strikeout rate separate from contact quality, with his contact quality cross-checked against Statcast expected results so a lucky defense doesn't flatter him. And since your batter only faces the starter two or three times, the opposing bullpen covers the remaining at-bats.

Statcast luck check (xBA) — expected batting average from how hard and where he hits the ball, i.e. what his contact should be earning. When his real average is above his xBA, the results have outrun the contact (running hot — we trim). When it's below, his contact deserves more than it's gotten (shortchanged — we bump, because results tend to catch up to contact).

Pitcher & platoon — a pitcher who gives up lots of hits raises the score; a tough one lowers it. We also use how the batter hits that pitcher's hand (lefty vs righty), which is a real, repeatable edge.

Park, weather & form — hitter-friendly parks and warm air nudge it up; recent form is a small factor because hot streaks mostly just reflect good hitters. Batter-vs-pitcher history is shown but barely counted — the samples are too small to trust.

The thing that matters most isn't any stat: it's whether he's actually in the lineup. Cards say Confirmed or Projected. Lineups firm up 1–3 hours before first pitch. Not betting advice.
